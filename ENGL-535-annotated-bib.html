Annotated Bibliography

Bennett, K.C. (2025). Rights-Based Ethics. In D.G. Ross (Ed.), The Routledge handbook of ethics in technical and professional communication (pp. 116-126) Routledge, Taylor & Francis Group. https://doi.org/10.4324/9781003434375

“Rights-Based Ethics” by Kristin C. Bennett argues that technical and professional communicators ("TPCers") must move beyond reliance on legal compliance and instead implement a rights-based ethical framework grounded in human dignity, autonomy, and social justice. She locates rights-based ethics within deontological (duty- or rules-based) theory and the UN Declaration of Human Rights, including "affirming an individuals' [sic] rights, such as the right to life, liberty and security, right to privacy, right to be free of discrimination, and right to freedom of expression." However, she contends that law and technical communication, though often considered to be neutral, can reinforce dominant power structures and marginalize nondominant identities, even when the intent is to promote inclusion.

The chapter traces TPC’s engagement with human rights, legal and social justice scholarship, emphasizing that documentation and policy writing shape public norms and affect access. Bennett claims that laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act "facilitate increased access" while reinforcing "dominant identities as normal." She discusses this in the context of Disney’s 2024 Disability Access Service policy revision which designated certain categories of disabilities which could access services, leaving out many others.

Bennett discusses the following critical issues and topics identified by TPC scholarship:

  • attending to the intersections between TPC and human rights,
  • facilitating legal literacy,
  • recognizing the law's globally dynamic nature, and
  • interrogating the law through intersectionality and social justice.

She proposes the following framework by which TPC practitioners can engage with rights-based ethics:

  • cultivate legal literacy,
  • understand federal and international laws and policies,
  • center human rights, and
  • work with and beyond the law through social justice.

Colton, J.S. (2025). Metaethics, technology, and the value of ethics in technical and professional communication. In D.G. Ross (Ed.), The Routledge handbook of ethics in technical and professional communication (pp. 77-84) Routledge, Taylor & Francis Group. https://doi.org/10.4324/9781003434375

“Metaethics, Technology, and the Value of Ethics in Technical and Professional Communication,” by Jared S. Colton argues that technical and professional communicators must engage not only ethical frameworks but also the metaethical assumptions that underly them. He contrasts normative ethics (how we should be) with descriptive ethics (how we are) and then defines metaethics as asking questions like "what is goodness?" and "how can we know right and wrong?" The goal of the chapter is to demonstrate that asking these questions changes how we understand scenarios, from assigning blame to identifying who is affected by the possible actions.

Using the example of generative AI (ChatGPT), Colton attempts to demonstrate the value of asking three questions:

  • Who or what counts as a moral agent?
  • How do we determine moral responsibility?
  • Why do ethical frameworks matter?

Colton claims that the answers to these kinds of questions are needed in order to advocate for better practices or behaviors. For example, as TPC professionals continue to work with generative AI, we will need to ask metaethical questions more often, even if the answers are unclear, to determine the relative contributions of humans and AI when assigning blame or credit for outcomes.

Colton encourages us to think about the metaethical approaches of multiple frameworks, which can shift the focus of an analysis, and suggests a combining approaches to reveal insights that a single ethical framework might not. He believes that, in addition to informing us about our values, metaethical reflection can open us to novel interactions with technology, TPC, and the world. Technical and professional communicators should embrace how different ethical frameworks help us understand daily ethical concerns, the way we reason about actions, and how to encourage certain forms of ethical thinking and behavior.

Pihlaja, B. (2025). Duty/deontological ethics. In D.G. Ross (Ed.), The Routledge handbook of ethics in technical and professional communication (pp. 94-105) Routledge, Taylor & Francis Group. https://doi.org/10.4324/9781003434375

In “Duty/Deontological Ethics,” Beau Pihlaja explains that, as a normative ethical framework, deontological ethics evaluates actions based on rules and principles rather than consequences. Pihlaja explains consequentialism, or considering the consequences of actions to understand their ethical value, as the main alternative approach to normative frameworks. He discusses the difficulty in defining duties separately from the outcomes of actions.

Pihlaja also mentions Kant's distinguishing between "perfect" and "imperfect" duties; the idea of imperfect duties points to the need for nuance despite the categorical imperative. The chapter discusses "big problems" in deontology, including the fact that case studies like the trolley problem seem removed from the experience of most people. However, when writing about applications of technology which could cause significant harm to large populations or the environment, ethical questions like "should this thing exist at all" or "to what ends might this technology/policy be used" become relevant.

The two main challenges to deontology -- appeal to authority (to determine duties) and the problem of epistemology -- are discussed.

Justification is important because it affects the actions we take in the world and how we relate to each other after we've acted. Pihlaja suggests that the appeal of deontological ethics is that "they might provide some measure of psychological comfort in a complex world." If we act according to duty, under deontology, we have acted ethically regardless of the outcome.

The problem of epistemology is "how we know what we know" when trying to think about the ethics of our actions. Case studies like the trolley problem assume all aspects of the situation are known, while this is rarely true in real life and "the messy complexity of existence" must ultimately be reckoned with in solving ethical dilemmas.

The problems of epistemology and of appeal to authority are linked; given the proliferation of information and misinformation, it can be harder to decide what ethical rules apply, and if "no one can really know anything," is there really any point to ethical reflection? Pihlaja states, "Adopting an unwavering 'deontological' or 'consequentialist' ethical stance will not serve us well" but neither will "simply doing what is most expedient in each moment." He claims that "having this language to talk about the consequences" of deontology and consequentialism enables TPC practitioners to evaluate whether and how we are acting ethically.

Pihlaja concludes that, because we live in the moment, we might not be able to resolve the tension between deontology and consequentialism in TPC work, but we can use both frameworks to help us cope with uncertainty (deontology) and do our best to act ethically in pursuit of the best outcomes (consequentialism).

Prabhakaran, V., Mitchell, M., Gebru, T., & Gabriel, I. (2022). A human rights-based approach to responsible AI. In 2022 ACM conference on equity and access in algorithms, mechanisms and optimization. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2210.02667

In "A Human Rights–Based Approach to Responsible AI," Prabhakaran et al argue that responsible AI (RAI) frameworks centered on abstract principles such as fairness, accountability, and transparency are insufficient without grounding in internationally recognized human rights standards. The authors claim that ethical research in AI is focused on the West, even though it is intended to apply to technologies that serve "a global population from diverse societies...with their own forms of injustices." They encourage moving away from a focus on "machines and the risks of their biases" with a human rights–based approach that offers a legal and moral foundation rooted in "a globally salient and cross-culturally recognized set of values." They discuss the need for "a common vocabulary and shared understanding between computer scientists and civil society" which is "crucial to bridge the gap between research and practice." It seems obvious that TPC professionals will be working to build this bridge and "the doctrine of human rights can serve as a starting point to help address some of these gaps."

A human rights-based framework plays a legal role in identifying AI-related harms; however, the authors identify three additional functions this framework performs. As a set of moral claims, human rights have intercultural and cross-cultural validity; they describe the overlapping nature of the responsibilities of states, organizations, and individuals; and they provide a shared vocabulary that TPC practitioners can invoke to address the concerns of people impacted by AI.

They distinguish three different aspects of the human rights framework:

  • a set of moral claims (human life has value, aspects of personhood must be protected; we must build a world where everyone can enjoy goods like physical security, health and education)
  • a legal regime and set of instruments (e.g. individuals' right to life, liberty, security, privacy, freedom of expression, freedom from discrimination)
  • a cultural practice and global social movement that focuses on advocacy, empowerment, and critique of existing institutions

The authors then apply three specific rights from the Universal Human Rights Declaration to "illustrate how human rights might shape responsible AI development." They discuss a human rights framework as a basis for cross-cultural value alignment, as a way of understanding responsibilities and duties, and as a framework for civil society participation, and present examples of how a human rights framework can add to the discussion of responsible AI. The human right to freedom from discrimination can address instances of bias in algorithmic systems. The human right to health can inform discussion of the relationship between healthcare and AI, by recognizing AI as not only a source of potential harm, but also as a source of improvements in health care services. The human right to share in scientific advancement could influence research to take into account the needs of, and share its benefits, with all communities.

Prabhakaran et al then suggest directions for future research into human rights-based approaches to AI ethics. Translational research could explain more precisely how human rights principles map to current ethics-based considerations in AI. Using the example of content moderation, they suggest that functional research could identify rights holders, what rights are at risk, and where and how rights conflict. Future research could also enable AI technologies to enhance access to goods like health care and education, especially for disadvantaged populations.

St. Amant, K., & Rife, M. C. (2010). Legal issues in global contexts: Reconsidering content in an age of globalization. Technical Communication, 57(3), 249-250https://doi.org/10.4324/9781315231440.

In "Legal Issues in Global Contexts: Reconsidering Content in an Age of Globalization," authors St. Amant and Rife argue that despite advances in technology, significant legal and cultural barriers continue to shape how content is created, shared, and managed globally. They challenge the notion that “the world is flat,” arguing instead that friction points exist, often as human constructs that reflect differing cultural perspectives, especially in the work of creating and managing content.

The editorial emphasizes that copyright law is a major obstacle to sharing and using content internationally. property vary substantially by jurisdiction. Differences in privacy regulations like those between the United States and the European Union affect how personal data is collected, transmitted, or archived. Language requirements, including mandates to translate product-related materials into official national languages, further complicate global communication practices.

According to the authors, because technical communicators are central to creating informational and instructional content, we are increasingly aware of and dealing with international legal discrepancies. TPC practitioners therefore can help organizations develop solutions to these areas of friction in global communication. To do this, we should combine our experience in content creation and management with our knowledge of legal issues that affect our practice. This knowledge and experience provides job security and opportunities for advancement for TPC professionals.

Williams, M. F. (2022). Gun control and gun rights: A conceptual framework for analyzing public policy issues in technical and professional communication. Technical Communication Quarterly, 31(1), 33-43. https://doi.org/10.1080.10572252.2021.1963487.

In "Gun Control and Gun Rights: A Conceptual Framework for Analyzing Public Policy Issues in Technical and Professional Communication," author Miriam F. Williams argues that TPC scholars and practitioners need to have a clear understanding of their role in public policy development. She presents a framework to help technical communicators identify how their work contributes to public policy development and implementation.

The model Williams is proposing, called the PRS conceptual model, helps TPC practitioners undertake this evaluation.

PRS stands for policy, role, and sites. Regarding policy, the framework asks TPC professionals to understand the policy development process in practice and the policy issue at various stages of development by:

  • identifying the problem and the stakeholders
  • collecting information about the policy's history
  • identifying the current policy and policies proposed by various stakeholders
  • understanding how current policies are implemented and regulated
  • identifying who is complying with or resisting current policy, and how
  • identifying how stakeholders are evaluating current policies

The policy phase is interative through the life of the analysis being undertaken.

The role phase explores technical communicators' role in policy writing and distribution as advocates for stakeholders. This stage should include trust-building strategies and requires identifying:

  • how TPC contributes to different stages of the development process
  • the methods for data collection and analysis
  • strategies for improvement to documents/design/processes
  • stakeholders who need advocacy
  • ethical and social justice frameworks that apply

The sites phase identifies where research on the above two points will take place and populations for inquiry and data collection. It requires consideration of TPC artifacts and populations/stakeholders who use, are affected by, or help create the artifacts under study.

Using the example of gun rights and gun control, the author provides examples of how to apply her model to a real-life problem. She claims that ethical deliberation about gun control requires balancing individual liberty with collective safety, recognizing that rights operate within social systems structured by mutual vulnerability and interdependence.

The PRS model does not prescribe theoretical perspectives or research methods for these studies. These decisions will be informed by the research question and levels of analysis, which likely overlap.

The PRS model can make public policy analysis accessible and provide a sense of comfort about policy issues some may not feel able to address in the workplace, community or classroom. It can help students recognize connections between real-life public policy issues and TPC, and identify high-stakes policy issues that TPC research hasn't covered.
