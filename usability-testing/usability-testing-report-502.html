Audience Analysis

The primary audience for the Student Forms page is UNM students.

Secondary audiences include

These are broad nonspecialist audiences with many subsets, and there will be real challenges in designing a user experience that meets their needs. According to Bettes, "Audience analysis can become complicated when you consider that you may have a combination of audience types and backgrounds: mixed audience types, wide variability within audience, and/or unknown audiences." She continues, "If you write to the lowest common denominator of reader, you are likely to end up with a cumbersome, tedious, book-like report that will turn off the majority of readers. However, if you do not write to that lowest level, you lose that segment of readers."

UNM's student body encompasses a wide variety of ages (though a significant proportion is likely to be Gen Z), nationalities, ethnicities, native languages, education levels, socioeconomic backgrounds, and life and work experience. The group of people who might provide assistance to students encountering difficulties with the forms is similarly diverse and difficult to characterize. The statement from Perelman and Olbrechts-Tyteca (quoted by Blakeslee) that "knowing an audience with certainty is impossible" is relevant and reassuring for this concern.

In its marketing, UNM identifies being a Hispanic-serving institution and focusing on first-generation students as important characteristics. These audience segments should be examined for particular needs, barriers, or other factors that could influence their interaction with online forms. For example, first-generation students don't always have a family member who understands the mundane details of college attendance like navigating registration requirements. Students whose family members don't read English might also have difficulty getting support to complete complex forms.

In Houser's discussion of Schriver's three audience analysis methods, he notes that in the intuition-driven method "a writer's experience with audience and texts will result in an improved product because the imagined audience in the writer's head will guide decisions made about the final product." My many years of experience with web development and UNM systems and processes will help guide audience research and recommendations about the user experience of the Student Forms page. In terms of identifying a shared "personal culture" for such diverse audiences, I could hypothesize that students and/or their helpers value education, and they want to follow rules and solve problems, since they are motivated to understand university regulations and processes and complete a required form to support the student's success (as opposed to ignoring the student's problem or giving up on trying to solve it). This may seem obvious, but the tone of the registrar's pages and forms (and UNM's websites more broadly) is often bureaucratic at best and oppositional at worst, sometimes almost working against audience members instead of with them. Language that is more welcoming, acknowledging the user's desire to comply with university regulations, might create a friendlier, more engaging experience which could even influence the user's opinion of the university as a whole.

Given that a higher percentage of the audience may be teens, with little to no formal work experience, who may or may not have trusted adults who can provide assistance, a focus on the "lowest level" seems like a good choice. More experienced users can skip the instructions if they don't need them, and simplifying form language and interfaces will help seasoned users as well as novices complete their task successfully without difficulty or additional guidance.

Method

Remote moderated testing will be performed with three testers, asking them to think aloud as they proceed through the test session. The test will incorporate some aspects of formative testing in that it will have a small sample size. The moderator will identify and note problems encountered by the test participants. Because the product is live and in use, however, the test will also have summative aspects, in that one goal is to determine whether the design and information architecture of the site successfully communicate information the target audience is seeking, and facilitate the completion of the task the user arrived at the site to accomplish.

Purpose and goals of test

I will analyze the design of the forms landing page and the user journey to completing the Demographic Information Change form, and make recommendations for improvements to the design, user experience, language and accessibility of the page and all its linked forms, using the Demographic Information Change form as an example. An an attempt will be made to identify potential internal obstacles to achieving those recommendations.

  • Determine whether participants can find, and understand how to complete, a form on the Student Forms page of the UNM Registrar's Office
  • Understand obstacles encountered by users
  • Identify recommendations for improving the user experience and usability of the site
Location and equipment

Participants used the device and web browser of their choice to conduct the test. One test was conducted in person in my home office in Albuquerque. Two tests were conducted online via Zoom. All tests were recorded using audio and/or video and were transcribed using the Office 365 MS Word Transcribe feature.

Participant recruitment

Testers are classmates assigned to the project; therefore, formal recruitment is not needed. Test scenario and task

The following scenario will be read to the participants before presenting the predefined, specific, open-ended task:

“Imagine you are a UNM student whose name has changed. You were directed to this page when you asked how to officially change the name the University has on record for you.”

Each participant will receive this task to complete.

“Please talk through the steps, as you are executing them, to complete the task of changing your name on official university records. Since the site is live, please do not actually complete the task, but indicate what steps you would take if you needed to complete it.”
